Lum Sohpetbneng, lording over the man-made Umiam lake about 20 kilometres short of
Meghalaya’s capital Shillong, is one of the most revered peaks for the matrilineal Khasi
tribe. Sohpetbneng, meaning ‘navel of heaven’, is believed to have had a mythical umbil
-ical cord connecting heaven and earth before human pride and sin severed the link. Eve
-ry second Sunday of February, the Khasi community, adhering to the indigenous faith,
trek 7 km to the peak, pledging to keep their unique traditions alive.

Everything else that bears the peak’s name in Meghalaya is expected to be sacred. Occ
-upying an uneven glade surrounded by pine trees, the Sohpetbneng Hostel is no longer
ethereal. It used to be a coveted accommodation for some 500 postgraduate students of
the North Eastern Hill University (NEHU) at Umshing-Mawkynroh on the outskirts of
Shillong.

Today, the hostel blocks stand like haunted houses, the walls and roofs covered with mo
ss and an outgrowth of plants. Chhinlung, the adjoining hostel named after a mythological
place believed to be the ancestral origin of the Mizo and Chin people, looks similar.
